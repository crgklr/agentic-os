Powell Sends Strongest Signal Yet That Interest Rate Cuts Are Coming

Jerome Powell said the "balance of risks" across the economy had started to shift, raising the odds the Federal Reserve lowers borrowing costs in September.

By Jeanna Smialek Aug. 22, 2025 6 min read

Jerome Powell, the Federal Reserve chair, at a news conference in Washington on Wednesday.

Jason Andrew for The New York Times

Jerome Powell, the Federal Reserve chair, delivered his strongest signal yet on Wednesday that the central bank is ready to cut interest rates, saying that the "balance of risks" across the economy had started to shift and that the Fed was "getting back on two-way street."

Mr. Powell's comments, made during a news conference after the Fed's July meeting, were widely interpreted as a sign that the central bank is preparing to lower borrowing costs at its next meeting in September. The Fed left rates unchanged at this meeting, as expected, keeping them at a range of 5.25 to 5.5 percent.

"We're getting back on two-way street," Mr. Powell said, referring to the Fed's ability to both raise and lower rates as economic conditions change. "We're no longer in a place where it's just about how high to raise rates."

The Fed chair's remarks came as the economy shows signs of cooling after a period of strong growth. Inflation has moderated from its peak last year, and the labor market, while still strong, has shown some signs of easing.

"The balance of risks has shifted," Mr. Powell said. "We're now in a position where we can be more patient and see how the data evolves."

Financial markets reacted positively to Mr. Powell's comments, with stocks rising and bond yields falling. The S&P 500 index gained 1.2 percent, while the yield on the 10-year Treasury note fell to 4.15 percent from 4.25 percent.

Analysts said Mr. Powell's remarks were the clearest indication yet that the Fed is preparing to pivot from its aggressive rate-hiking campaign, which began in March 2022, to a more accommodative stance.

"This is a significant shift in tone," said Diane Swonk, chief economist at KPMG. "Powell is essentially saying that the Fed has done enough to fight inflation and is now ready to support the economy if needed."

The Fed's next meeting is scheduled for Sept. 17-18, and many economists now expect the central bank to cut rates by a quarter percentage point at that meeting.

However, Mr. Powell emphasized that the Fed would remain data-dependent and that any rate cuts would depend on continued progress on inflation and the overall health of the economy.

"We're not on a preset course," he said. "We'll make decisions meeting by meeting based on the data."
